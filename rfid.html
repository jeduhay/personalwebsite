Expedition at Birch Aquarium

Birch Aquarium at Scripps aims to promote ocean conservation and education by serving as the public outreach center for Scripps Institute of Oceanography at University of California, San Diego. Opening 2018, Expedition will showcase the global efforts made by Scripps to engage visitors in understanding global phenomena at a human scale and human impact on a global scale.

RFID Pathways

From July – September 2017, I worked within a multidisciplinary team to research and design ways to facilitate interaction and learning at museum exhibits through the use of RFID technology. Under the guidance of EnVision Maker Studio and in collaboration with Birch Aquarium at Scripps, we were responsible for delivering design updates, budget and continuity reports, and a working prototype of an RFID-enabled expeditionary pathway experience. I served as the UI / UX lead of my team and performed the following tasks:

  • Front-end web development (HTML, CSS, JavaScript)
  • Graphic design (Adobe Photoshop, Adobe Illustrator)
  • Rapid prototyping (user testing, wireframing, heuristic evaluation)

Personalizing Aquarium Experiences Using RFID Technology

Museums and aquariums across the world are implementing new technological features to make their exhibits more interactive and engaging for visitors. Birch Aquarium seeks to transform their approach by utilizing radio-frequency identification (RFID) technology as an inexpensive yet effective way to immerse visitors into their research.

Upon entry into the aquarium, visitors may register their information and preferences to RFID cards that will allow each individual to receive personalized information while touring the exhibits.

How can exhibit content and interaction be improved to better suit the personal and educational needs of each individual visitor?


Step 1: Research

We conducted on-site research at Birch Aquarium and observed that few visitors actually read the existing exhibit displays. We asked around and learned the following:

  • The information was not relevant to their individual interests.
  • The displays were static and not fun to look at.
  • They would rather just look at the exhibit itself rather than read about it.

We then performed a competitive analysis on other technologically-driven museums and learned the following to address the issues at Birch:

  • Visitors feel more engaged when they are given the choice to learn what aligns most with their own interests and personal motives for visiting.
  • Interacting with displays is an effective way for visitors to learn and retain information.
  • After leaving the museum, visitors should be given the option to access the content on their own.

Takeaway #1: Visitor motivations are diverse. Some visitors may come to Birch Aquarium to learn about climate research in the Arctic, while others may visit to explore the biological organisms that exist in La Jolla Shores. Not everyone wants to see the same thing, but there's only so much information that Birch can convey in a limited digital space.

Professional Pathways



By giving visitors the option to select a professional persona, they will be able to view exhibit information relevant to their own motives and interests. Each exhibit display will house a ton of information, but when a visitor scans their RFID card, the display will show what they want to see first and foremost.

This allows Birch Aquarium to portray as much information as they want while still retaining visitor engagement.


Takeaway #2: Touch-screen displays are fun. In addition to scanning cards at displays, each existing display will be retrofitted to have touch and swipe functionality to allow non-linear navigation across text, videos, pictures, and quizzes. Non-linearity further provides user freedom and flexibility by allowing visitors to learn what they want to learn.




Takeaway #3: A web-application gets the job done. It can serve as a centralized hub that connects four subsystems: the registration kiosk, the exhibit displays, the administration control center, and the online database.

A web-application also allows visitors to access any information they like online.


Step 2: Information Architecture

We began building the web-application by dividing the information into three main separate components. The registration kiosk and the terminal – when displayed on monitors throughout the aquarium – are configured to be in kiosk mode to prevent users from accessing irrelevant material. The admin control center is remotely accessible for Birch staff to view and edit the content being shown.




Step 3: Wireframing and Iteration

The front-end design process involved creating wireframe sketches, implementing low-fidelity mockups, and iterating based on user testing and feedback.

Step 4: Final Prototype

In 10 weeks, we have delivered a working prototype of an RFID-enabled expeditionary pathway experience. We accomplished this by developing a web-application that registers users to a database, displays streamlined information from a collection of variations, and allows admins to edit content remotely.

However, we ended up allocating most of our time to developing the visitor-side UI / UX that we did not have enough time to polish the admin content editor. The web-application does allow admins to edit exhibit content, but the interface is only in its early stages.

This project was intended to be carried forward by another team, and all of our concerns, conclusions, and suggestions for future development can be seen in the continuity report at the bottom of this page.
